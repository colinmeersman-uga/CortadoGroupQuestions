## The Five Dysfunctions of a Team - Patrick Lencioni

Lencioni describes the importance of vulnerability in teams. As Kathryn begins her journey of getting her employees to open up, she encounters a decent amount discomfort within her team. In my own life, I have been thrown into numerous group projects in college where the only thing I know about my teammates is that they are in the same class as me and want to get a decent grade on their project. You have mentioned in the past that Cortado Group is a sort of heaven and that there will be aspects that I wish I could take with me to any place that I work. 

+ I am curious, do you feel that it is this vulerablity and trust dynamic that makes this company great or if there are other things that are greater contributers to your fondness of the company?

**DR:** Specifically, yes — it is the greatest contributor. The companies I have worked at that have been great were great because of the high level of trust, which requires vulnerability. If you are not transparent about your weaknesses, you do not allow someone else to cover those weaknesses and provide the most effective and thorough team that you could be. It is incredibly helpful that everyone on our team is very competent, but it is also meaningful that they have great intentions and a level of integrity that makes trust come easily.

Lencioni also describes having the same scoreboard for all team members. This was strange to me at first because I have always thought that different team members should have different goals that they want to achieve. For example, A salesperson being focused on revenue while a process engineer is focused on system efficiency. As Lencioni drew out his point though, I began to see what he meant. Everyone on an executive business team is responsible for marketing, finance, customer service, etc. This is because if an executives concern is only about their division, they will not see the health of the business as a whole. 

+ Does Cortado Group have a common "Scoreboard" that they use to measure success for the company? (New Client Acquisition? Revenue? Profit? Current GTM Strategy Results?)

**DR:** In general, we look at organizational growth as well as customer success metrics. We find it difficult to get customers to actually tell us how we are doing in the form of a customer satisfaction survey response, so we use other mechanisms as a proxy — such as follow-on work and references. Both of those result in organizational growth.

## Getting Naked - Patrick Lencioni

I agreed with most of the things Lencioni describes in his fable. One point that I disagree with is "Better to have no client than a bad one". The threefold reasoning behind this was that a bad client will blame the failure of performance on the service provider and leave them with poor reviews. Lencioni argues that the "bad client" will decrease employee morale for the people working that project. Lastly, if the business is involved in morally questionable activities that go aginst the mission statement of your company, they should be turned down as a client. I disagree with the first two points for a few reasons. I think what a "bad client" means needs to be better defined. If it is meant as a client who is unpleasant to work with, I feel that many prospective customers would be turned away. Each company is difficult in their own way. (Ex. Lack of Punctuality, abravisive personalities, unclear communication) Even if "Bad Client" was extended to mean untrustworthy and disrespectful, if a company really needs a client, they don't get to be picky on who they get. Also, most of the time, it is learned that a company isn't going to be pleasent to work with some time into the engagement. To drop the client after you have already begun to work for them would seem to explain the poor performance reviews recieved from these companies more than the fact that they were difficult. I am curious your thoughts on this question. 

+ Should a company turn down a customer if them deem them a "bad client" before the engagement?

**DR:** In general, if you can tell that they will be a less than optimum use of your resources, you have an opportunity loss for every bad client you take on. So if you know specifically that they are in a minefield associated with high delivery cost and low likelihood of success, even though they're willing to pay you money, either you charge them a coefficient associated with their lack of long-term value, or you turn them away. Sometimes, if they are particularly unpleasant, the impact on your organization's morale can be so bad that you actually end up losing very valuable people because you forced them to work with a miserable client.

Lencioni also makes a compelling case for vulnerability, particularly when he talks about shedding the 'Fear of Being Embarrassed.' He advises asking naive questions and readily admitting ignorance to build long-term trust. However, I am curious about how this applies at the very beginning of a relationship or early in a career. 

+ How do you balance this approach of extreme vulnerability with the need to establish a baseline of credibility and technical expertise so the client trusts you in the first place?

**DR:** Generally, following the "always be consulting" model in Getting Naked puts you in a place where you are continually vulnerable. You have the opportunity for them to simply take your free consulting and leave without ever paying you a dime. But you're counting on the fact that you have conveyed your value such that your credibility and expertise make you trustworthy — by being a voice of reason, and speaking to them the cold hard truths of reality they so desperately need. There really isn't a balance.

## The Infinite Game - Simon Sinek

I really enjoyed reading Simon Sinek's The Infinite Game. The framework he laid out resonated with me, particularly his emphasis on the importance of company flexibility, the value of identifying worthy rivals to push continuous improvement, and the absolute necessity of courageous leadership. While I aligned with the vast majority of his philosophy, there were a few specific points he made that I found myself questioning, and I'd love to get your perspective on them.

In Chapter 2, Sinek uses the scientists at the Leningrad seed bank as the ultimate example of a 'Just Cause.' He praises them for starving to death rather than eating the seeds they were protecting, arguing that their infinite mindset allowed them to sacrifice themselves for the future. While I completely align with a company needing a visionary Just Cause, I struggled with this specific boundary. 

+ When human lives are immediately at stake, shouldn't the finite game—survival—supersede the infinite game? In a business context, at what point does a relentless commitment to a future vision become a failure of leadership to protect the team in the present?

**DR:** In every case where human life is at stake, please hang up and dial 911. If you are in business, chances are very slim that you have human lives immediately at stake. You need to follow triage rules when human lives are directly at stake: which person can I spend time with to get them into a more survivable condition based upon the resources that we physically have available at this time? There is always a possibility that you can think so far ahead that you stumble into a pitfall or minefield that you weren't paying attention to. So it's super important to have operators and visionaries functioning at the same time, each with a span of vision in mind. Operationally, I find myself often wearing the hat where it's my responsibility to execute the vision created by others. I am lucky to be in the position where I get to contribute significantly to that vision and then execute on it. So I have to wear both sets of glasses, if you will.

I appreciated Sinek’s distinction between finite and infinite players, but I disagreed with his assertion that the US failed in the Vietnam War because they lacked an infinite mindset, and that adopting one would have changed the outcome. The reasoning Sinek provides is that the Viet Cong were fighting an infinite game for their survival, while the US was fighting a finite game focused on traditional metrics like territory and body counts. He argues that in a matchup between a finite and infinite player, the infinite player will always outlast the finite one. I disagree with applying this framework to military strategy. War, by its very nature, cannot be approached with an infinite mindset because it is strictly bound by finite resources (lives, money, and capital). No country has the capability to sustain a conflict indefinitely before those finite realities force an end. Sinek treats the "infinite mindset" as a strategic choice, but in environments governed by resource scarcity, that choice simply doesn't exist.

+ How can an organization realistically play the "infinite game" when its most critical resources (whether that is capital or public support for the war effort) are by nature finite?

**DR:** That really is the nature of a scarcity mindset. If you believe you cannot attract others to your cause, and if you believe that your resources are finite, they absolutely will be. When you recognize that you can attract others to your cause and motivate success in ways that exponentiate beyond what you currently recognize as your finite support, you end up being able to accomplish far more than you originally think with a scarcity mentality. The general concept is: hugs do not disappear simply because I give them away.
