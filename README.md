## Getting Naked - Patrick Lencioni

I agreed with most of the things Lencioni describes in his fable. One point that I disagree with is "Better to have no client than a bad one". The threefold reasoning behind this was that a bad client will blame the failure of performance on the service provider and leave them with poor reviews. Lencioni argues that the "bad client" will decrease employee morale for the people working that project. Lastly, if the business is involved in morally questionable activities that go aginst the mission statement of your company, they should be turned down as a client. I disagree with the first two points for a few reasons. I think what a "bad client" means needs to be better defined. If it is meant as a client who is unpleasant to work with, I feel that many prospective customers would be turned away. Each company is difficult in their own way. (Ex. Lack of Punctuality, abravisive personalities, unclear communication) Even if "Bad Client" was extended to mean untrustworthy and disrespectful, if a company really needs a client, they don't get to be picky on who they get. Also, most of the time, it is learned that a company isn't going to be pleasent to work with some time into the engagement. To drop the client after you have already begun to work for them would seem to explain the poor performance reviews recieved from these companies more than the fact that they were difficult. I am curious your thoughts on this question. Should a company turn down a customer if them deem them a "bad client" before the engagement?

Lencioni also makes a compelling case for vulnerability, particularly when he talks about shedding the 'Fear of Being Embarrassed.' He advises asking naive questions and readily admitting ignorance to build long-term trust. However, I am curious about how this applies at the very beginning of a relationship or early in a career. How do you balance this approach of extreme vulnerability with the need to establish a baseline of credibility and technical expertise so the client trusts you in the first place?

## The Infinite Game - Simon Sinek

I really enjoyed reading Simon Sinek's The Infinite Game. The framework he laid out resonated with me, particularly his emphasis on the importance of company flexibility, the value of identifying worthy rivals to push continuous improvement, and the absolute necessity of courageous leadership. While I aligned with the vast majority of his philosophy, there were a few specific points he made that I found myself questioning, and I'd love to get your perspective on them.

In Chapter 2, Sinek uses the scientists at the Leningrad seed bank as the ultimate example of a 'Just Cause.' He praises them for starving to death rather than eating the seeds they were protecting, arguing that their infinite mindset allowed them to sacrifice themselves for the future. While I completely align with a company needing a visionary Just Cause, I struggled with this specific boundary. When human lives are immediately at stake, shouldn't the finite game—survival—supersede the infinite game? In a business context, at what point does a relentless commitment to a future vision become a failure of leadership to protect the team in the present?

I appreciated Sinek’s distinction between finite and infinite players, but I disagreed with his assertion that the US failed in the Vietnam War because they lacked an infinite mindset, and that adopting one would have changed the outcome. The reasoning Sinek provides is that the Viet Cong were fighting an infinite game for their survival, while the US was fighting a finite game focused on traditional metrics like territory and body counts. He argues that in a matchup between a finite and infinite player, the infinite player will always outlast the finite one. I disagree with applying this framework to military strategy. War, by its very nature, cannot be approached with an infinite mindset because it is strictly bound by finite resources (lives, money, and capital). No country has the capability to sustain a conflict indefinitely before those finite realities force an end. Sinek treats the "infinite mindset" as a strategic choice, but in environments governed by resource scarcity, that choice simply doesn't exist.

How can an organization realistically play the "infinite game" when its most critical resources (whether that is capital or public support for the war effort) are by nature finite?
